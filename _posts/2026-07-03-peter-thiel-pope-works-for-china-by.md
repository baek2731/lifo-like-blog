Peter Thiel just said the quiet part out loud: his brand of tech libertarianism views any moral or ethical oversight as an attack.

The man who co-founded PayPal, funded Facebook, and is basically Silicon Valley royalty, now thinks Pope Francis is a Beijing operative because the Pontiff dared to suggest AI needs some guardrails. If you thought the culture war couldn't get any weirder, Thiel's here to prove you wrong.

## Thiel's Papal Decree: AI Ethics as Communist Propaganda

Thiel, speaking at the conservative National Conservatism Conference in Brussels, didn't just disagree with the Pope. He accused him of "working for the Chinese Communists." This isn't a nuanced critique of AI policy; it's a full-blown conspiracy theory wrapped in a tech-bro bow.

The Pope has been consistent on AI. He calls for ethical development, human dignity, and protection against algorithmic bias. This isn't exactly groundbreaking stuff; it's the kind of sentiment you hear from ethicists at Google, Microsoft, and pretty much every academic institution wrestling with AI.

But to Thiel, "global ethics" is a Trojan horse. He sees calls for regulation as a deliberate attempt to slow Western AI development, giving China an advantage. It's the ultimate Cold War framing: either you're with American tech exceptionalism, or you're a communist sympathizer.

This isn't a new playbook for Thiel. He's been warning about China's rise and the supposed complacency of the West for years. He argues that America is "sleepwalking" into a tech defeat, and any internal dissent or ethical hand-wringing only weakens the resolve.

### The Vatican's AI Stance: Not Exactly Maoist

Let's unpack what the Vatican actually advocates. They've partnered with IBM and Microsoft on the "Rome Call for AI Ethics," a document pushing for transparency, non-discrimination, and accountability in AI systems. It's a fairly standard list of ethical principles, focusing on human-centric AI.

They talk about "algorethics" – the ethical considerations of algorithms. They're worried about things like AI being used for mass surveillance, job displacement without social safety nets, and the erosion of human autonomy. These are legitimate concerns, not some secret plan to usher in a communist utopia.

Thiel's argument hinges on the idea that *any* regulation, *any* ethical pause, inherently slows innovation. For him, the only way to win the AI race against China is to sprint headlong, unburdened by moral qualms. It's a "move fast and break things" philosophy applied to geopolitics, with the whole world as the debugging environment.

I've seen this play out before, countless times. Every new technology, from biotech to crypto, gets its messianic champions who insist that any attempt to slow it down is an assault on progress itself. Then, inevitably, the downsides emerge, and we're left scrambling to patch up the societal holes.

## The Geopolitics of Code: Thiel's China Obsession

Thiel’s China fixation isn’t just about tech; it’s a deep-seated ideological battle. He views China as an existential threat, not just an economic competitor. His venture capital firm, Founders Fund, has reportedly barred investments in Chinese startups, taking a hardline stance. This isn't just about market share; it's about two fundamentally different visions of the future.

He believes China is leveraging AI for authoritarian control and that the West needs to counter this by accelerating its own AI development, unimpeded. Any internal debate about ethics or regulation is, in his view, a form of self-sabotage. It's a zero-sum game.

The Reddit community, specifically r/technology, is having a field day with this. You see comments ranging from "Thiel has officially lost his mind" to "Is he really trying to own the libs by calling the Pope a commie?" Others are pointing out the irony of a billionaire lecturing about global power dynamics while funding surveillance companies. A few, predictably, are echoing Thiel's sentiment, arguing that "woke ethics" are indeed slowing down American innovation.

### AI Ethics: A Global Conversation, Not a Chinese Plot

The push for AI ethics is a global phenomenon. The European Union is leading the charge with its AI Act, a comprehensive regulatory framework. The United States, while slower to legislate, has seen major tech companies and academic institutions release their own ethical guidelines. This isn't a conspiracy; it's a necessary evolution as AI becomes more powerful and pervasive.

Thiel's rhetoric is a distraction from the real, complex challenges of AI. It simplifies a multifaceted issue into a black-and-white struggle, attempting to silence dissenting voices by painting them as traitors or dupes. This approach is dangerous, not just for AI development, but for public discourse itself.

### The Real Stakes: Beyond Thiel's Provocations

The actual debate around AI ethics involves incredibly nuanced questions. How do we ensure AI doesn't perpetuate or amplify existing societal biases? What happens to jobs when AI automates vast swathes of the economy? How do we prevent autonomous weapons from escalating conflicts? How do we secure data and privacy in an AI-powered world? These aren't trivial concerns.

Companies like Google have faced significant internal strife over ethical AI, leading to high-profile firings and resignations. This isn't because a handful of Google employees are secretly working for Beijing. It's because the technology is powerful, and its implications are profound. Ignoring the ethical dimension is not just naive; it's irresponsible. See the detailed analysis by *Ars Technica* on Google's AI ethics struggles.

Thiel's argument that "ethics" is a communist plot to slow the West is a convenient way to dismiss any criticism of unchecked technological power. It allows him and others who share his views to avoid the hard questions, to bypass accountability, and to push for a future where technology develops without meaningful human oversight.

Having covered the tech industry for over a decade, I've seen this pattern repeat: a powerful new technology emerges, its proponents promise utopia, and anyone raising concerns is labeled a Luddite, a socialist, or now, apparently, a Chinese operative. It's an old tactic to shut down legitimate debate.

The global conversation around AI ethics is about building a future where technology serves humanity, rather than the other way around. It's about ensuring that the immense power of AI is wielded responsibly, with human dignity at its core. To frame this as a communist plot is not just intellectually dishonest; it's deeply cynical.

## What's Really Driving Thiel's Rhetoric?

Thiel's comments are less about the Pope's actual stance on AI and more about signaling to his base: the hardline conservatives and libertarian tech enthusiasts who distrust global institutions and see any regulation as an overreach. He's playing to the gallery, reinforcing a narrative where the West is under constant threat and only a radical, uncompromising approach can save it.

He's not just criticizing the Pope; he's criticizing the very idea of a global ethical framework. For Thiel, power is paramount, and ethics are a weakness in a world engaged in a technological arms race. This isn't about the specific content of the Vatican's AI ethics document; it's about the principle that anyone *should* be dictating how technology develops, especially if it slows down "his side."

This rhetoric also serves to justify the rapid, often unchecked development of AI technologies by companies Thiel invests in or influences. If ethical concerns are just communist propaganda, then there's no need to slow down, no need for extensive safety testing, no need for public debate. Just build, build, build.

It's a convenient narrative for those who stand to gain most from a hands-off approach to AI regulation. It allows them to externalize the costs and risks of their innovations onto society, while reaping the benefits. For more context on Thiel's broader political influence, a *Reuters* piece detailed his significant impact on conservative politics.

### The Echo Chamber Effect: From Brussels to Reddit

The impact of such statements, especially from someone as influential as Thiel, is significant. It filters down, gets amplified in certain media circles, and contributes to the polarization of the AI debate. It shifts the discussion from *how* to build ethical AI to *whether* ethical AI is even a legitimate goal.

On Reddit, this kind of hot take is catnip. It generates outrage, agreement, and endless threads of debate. It entrenches people further into their existing positions. The nuance of the Vatican's actual position is lost, replaced by the soundbite of "Pope is a communist."

This is how important, complex technological debates get derailed. Instead of a sober discussion about the risks and rewards of AI, we get hyperbolic accusations and ideological purity tests. It’s not productive, but it sure gets clicks and reinforces tribal loyalties.

The real danger here isn't the Pope's ethical guidelines. It's the attempt to weaponize rhetoric, to demonize legitimate concerns, and to suppress a crucial global conversation about the future of a technology that will reshape every aspect of human life.

## Thiel's Old Testament: Power, Control, and AI

Thiel's comments aren't an outlier; they're consistent with his long-held philosophy. He's a proponent of "techno-optimism" mixed with a heavy dose of realpolitik. He views the world through a lens of competition and power, where technological superiority is the ultimate arbiter.

The idea that religious institutions or international bodies could have a legitimate say in how technology is developed fundamentally clashes with his libertarian-inflected worldview. He sees these bodies as impediments to progress, as forces that would rather level the playing field than allow American innovation to dominate.

This isn't about AI ethics; it's about control. Who gets to decide the future of AI? Is it the unelected tech moguls and venture capitalists, or is it a broader coalition of governments, ethicists, religious leaders, and civil society? Thiel's answer is clear: the tech elite, unencumbered by "global ethics."

It’s a stark choice he’s presenting: unbridled AI development, or communist-sympathizing stagnation. And if you're not with him, you're against him. It's a classic Thiel move, designed to provoke, to simplify, and to consolidate power. The Pope just happened to be in the crosshairs this time.
